## 1. Peak-End Rule: Structure Your 3 Minutes Around Two Moments

### The Mechanism

Daniel Kahneman and colleagues demonstrated that people evaluate past experiences based on two moments: the **peak intensity** moment and the **final moment** -- not the average or the duration. This was established through the cold-water experiments (Kahneman, Fredrickson, Schreiber & Redelmeier, 1993) where participants preferred a longer painful experience (60s cold water + 30s slightly warmer) over a shorter one (60s cold water alone), because the ending was better.

**Duration neglect** means that the length of an experience barely factors into evaluation. A 3-minute pitch and a 5-minute pitch are judged almost identically in memory -- what matters is the peak and the end.

Key study: Redelmeier & Kahneman (1996) showed this extends to real medical procedures. Patients who had a less painful final 30 seconds of a colonoscopy rated the ENTIRE procedure as less painful, even though it was objectively longer.

### Application to EDEN's 3-Minute Pitch

**Structure recommendation: The "Double Peak" Architecture**

```
0:00-0:30  -- HOOK (first impression, separate mechanism, see section 9)
0:30-1:00  -- Context setup (Mars, 4 astronauts, the problem)
1:00-1:30  -- Capability demonstration (dashboard, agent system)
1:30-2:15  -- PEAK MOMENT: The CME crisis hits. LEDs change. Countdown begins.
             The plant is visibly "stressed." The agent council debates.
             This is your emotional high-water mark.
2:15-2:45  -- Resolution: The system saves the crew. Show the result.
2:45-3:00  -- THE END: Not a summary. A resonant final image/statement.
             "This is what autonomous intelligence looks like when
             failure means people don't eat."
```

**Critical tactical insight:** Most teams put their best content in the middle and trail off with "future work" or "what we learned." This is catastrophically wrong. The last 15 seconds are disproportionately weighted in memory. End on your strongest emotional note, not logistics.

**Specific EDEN tactic:** The LEDs returning to full brightness (plant "recovering") should be the LAST thing judges see. Visual recovery = positive end = higher retrospective evaluation of entire pitch.

## 2. Cognitive Fatigue in Sequential Judging

### The Mechanism

The landmark study is Danziger, Levav & Avnaim-Pesso (2011), published in PNAS, which analyzed 1,112 judicial rulings by Israeli parole boards. Favorable rulings dropped from ~65% at the start of a session to nearly 0% before a break, then reset to ~65% after a break. Fatigued judges defaulted to the **status quo** (deny parole = safe choice).

Baumeister's ego depletion model (Baumeister, Bratslavsky, Muraven & Tice, 1998) explains why: decision-making uses a finite cognitive resource. After many decisions, people:

1. **Default to the status quo** -- the safe, expected choice
2. **Use simplifying heuristics** -- "does this FEEL right?" rather than analytical evaluation
3. **Weight easily-processed information more heavily** -- simple > complex
4. **Rely on affect heuristic** -- "how do I feel about this?" becomes the answer to "how good is this?"
5. **Anchor more heavily to first impressions** -- the thin-slice sticks because they lack energy to revise

### The Specific Heuristics Tired Judges Use

After team 15 of 20+, judges are running on:

- **Recognition heuristic**: "Have I seen something like this before?" If yes, categorize and move on. If no, pay attention.
- **Affect heuristic** (Slovic et al., 2002): "How does this make me FEEL?" replaces "How good IS this technically?"
- **Anchoring**: The first number, claim, or impression sticks. They won't recalculate.
- **Availability heuristic**: Whatever is easiest to recall = most important. Vivid > abstract.
- **Confirmation bias**: Once they form an initial impression (positive or negative), they selectively attend to confirming evidence.

### Exploiting This Ethically

**If you present late in the order (team 15+):**
- Your #1 enemy is not low quality -- it is being forgettable and getting the "default" average score
- Be EASY TO PROCESS. Tired judges punish complexity. One clear story beats five features.
- Trigger AFFECT, not ANALYSIS. They literally cannot analyze carefully anymore. Make them feel something.
- Be NOVEL (see section 6). Novelty is the one thing that overrides fatigue -- the orienting response is involuntary.

**If you present early (team 1-5):**
- Judges are analytical. Lead with substance.
- But your scores may be slightly conservative (judges "save room" for later teams).

**If you present mid-order:**
- Worst position. Judges are starting to fatigue but haven't given up yet. They're comparing you to the best they've seen so far.

**Specific EDEN tactic:** Regardless of position, use the physical plant prop. It breaks the pattern of screen-only presentations, triggers the orienting response (involuntary attention to novelty), and engages the affect heuristic through sensory experience rather than analytical evaluation.

## 3. The Water Cooler Test: What Makes Judges Talk About You

### The Mechanism

Memorability research converges on several mechanisms:

**Von Restorff Effect / Isolation Effect** (1933): Items that are distinctly different from their surroundings are remembered better. In a list of words, the one printed in red is recalled first. In a hackathon, the team with the physical plant is the red word.

**Emotional tagging** (McGaugh, 2004; Cahill & McGaugh, 1998): The amygdala modulates memory consolidation in the hippocampus. Experiences with emotional arousal are stored more durably. The emotion doesn't need to be extreme -- moderate arousal (surprise, delight, tension) is sufficient.

**Narrative structure** (Bruner, 1991): Humans remember stories, not facts. A team that presents a STORY is recalled as a story. A team that presents FEATURES is recalled as... nothing, because feature lists don't have retrieval hooks.

**Distinctiveness + Emotion + Narrative = Memorability**

### What Makes a Judge Say "Did You See That Team?"

Research on word-of-mouth (Berger, 2013 -- "Contagious") identifies 6 drivers of sharing (STEPPS):
1. **Social Currency** -- makes the sharer look smart for noticing it
2. **Triggers** -- environmental cues that remind you of it
3. **Emotion** -- high-arousal emotions (awe, excitement, anxiety) drive sharing
4. **Public** -- visible, observable elements
5. **Practical Value** -- useful information
6. **Stories** -- people share narratives, not facts

For hackathon judging, the most relevant are:
- **Social Currency**: "You should have seen this team -- they had an actual PLANT on stage that responded to their AI"
- **Emotion**: Awe/surprise from the physical demo
- **Stories**: "So there's this AI managing a Mars greenhouse, and a solar storm hits, and the plant literally starts dying in front of you..."

### Specific EDEN Tactic

The water cooler moment is: "They had an actual plant with lights that dimmed when the solar storm hit in their simulation." This is your viral hook. It's physical (memorable), surprising (breaks pattern), emotional (plant "dying" triggers biophilic concern), and narrative (it happens DURING a story). The fact that judges will describe this scene to each other is more valuable than any technical feature you could demo.

## 4. Embodied Cognition: The Power of Your Physical Plant

### The Mechanism

Embodied cognition research demonstrates that physical sensory experience fundamentally shapes abstract thought and judgment.

**Key studies:**

- **Ackerman, Nocera & Bargh (2010), Science**: Participants holding heavy clipboards rated job candidates as more serious and qualified. Holding rough objects made social interactions seem more difficult. Physical weight = psychological weight.

- **Williams & Bargh (2008), Science**: Briefly holding a warm cup of coffee (vs. iced coffee) led participants to rate a stranger as having a "warmer" personality. Physical warmth = social warmth.

- **Jostmann, Lakens & Schubert (2009)**: Heavy objects increased perceived importance and value of unrelated items. Weight triggers "importance" judgments.

- **Krishna (2012), Journal of Consumer Research**: Sensory marketing research shows multi-sensory product experiences increase perceived quality, willingness to pay, and brand memorability by 30-70% over visual-only presentations.

### What This Means for a Plant in a Sea of Screens

Every other team projects pixels. EDEN puts a living organism on the table. The implications from embodied cognition:

1. **Concreteness effect**: Abstract concepts presented alongside physical objects are judged as more real, important, and credible. "AI managing a greenhouse" + actual plant = "this is real." "AI managing a greenhouse" + dashboard only = "this is a concept."

2. **Biological affinity (biophilia)**: Wilson's biophilia hypothesis (1984) suggests humans have an innate attention bias toward living things. A living plant in a room full of screens will involuntarily draw gaze. fMRI studies confirm that viewing natural living things activates reward circuitry (Valtchanov & Ellard, 2015).

3. **Stakes concretization**: When the LEDs dim and the plant appears to "suffer," the abstract concept of "crop failure on Mars" becomes visceral. This isn't a simulation anymore -- there is a physical living thing right here whose fate is being decided.

4. **Touch and proximity**: If judges can see/nearly touch the plant, proximity to a physical object increases ownership psychology and caring (the "endowment effect" extends to mere proximity -- Reb & Connolly, 2007).

### Specific EDEN Tactic

- Place the plant on the judges' table if possible, or as close to them as you can get. Proximity matters.
- Do NOT explain the plant at the beginning. Let it sit there, drawing curiosity. Then reveal its role during the demo. The gap between "why is there a plant?" and "OH, it's connected to the AI" creates a curiosity gap (Loewenstein, 1994) that locks attention.
- When LEDs dim, say nothing for 1-2 seconds. Let judges see it happen. Silence + visual change is more powerful than narration during peak moments.

## 5. Narrative Transportation: How Stories Override Analysis

### The Mechanism

Green & Brock (2000) established the Transportation-Imagery Model: when someone is "transported" into a narrative, they:

1. **Reduce counter-arguing** -- they stop critically evaluating claims
2. **Experience real emotions** -- as if the events were happening to them
3. **Update beliefs** -- consistent with the story's conclusion
4. **Show halo effects** -- positive feelings about the story spread to ALL evaluations of the presenter

This was replicated across dozens of studies. Meta-analysis by Van Laer et al. (2014) confirmed: narrative transportation leads to more favorable attitudes, intentions, and beliefs, with effect sizes of d = 0.40-0.70.

**Critical finding for hackathons**: When judges are "transported" into your narrative, they score higher on ALL dimensions -- not just presentation quality, but also perceived technical quality, creativity, and functionality. The halo is comprehensive.

### Triggers for Transportation (What Makes Someone Get Absorbed)

Green & Brock identified key antecedents:

1. **Identifiable characters** -- not "users" but specific people with names and stakes
2. **Vivid imagery** -- sensory details that create mental simulation
3. **Suspense/uncertainty** -- "will they make it?" tension
4. **Emotional stakes** -- what happens if it fails?
5. **Causal logic** -- events must feel connected, not random

Slater & Rouner (2002) added: **reduced perceived persuasive intent**. If the audience feels they're being "sold to," transportation breaks. If they feel they're experiencing a story, defenses drop.

### How to Trigger Transportation in 3 Minutes

**The EDEN narrative transportation script:**

```
CHARACTERS:     "Commander Chen and her crew of four, 280 days into
                a 450-day Mars mission"
                (Specific name + specific number = identifiable)

VIVID IMAGERY:  "The greenhouse is their only source of fresh food.
                 Every tomato matters. Every leaf is counted."
                 (Sensory, concrete, stakes are physical)

SUSPENSE:       "Then DONKI flags a coronal mass ejection. 50 hours
                 until it hits. What does the AI do?"
                 (Question format creates open loop in judge's mind)

EMOTIONAL STAKE: "If the greenhouse fails, they're on emergency
                  rations for 170 days. That's not a bug report.
                  That's hunger."
                  (Escalation from technical to human)

CAUSAL LOGIC:   Show the AI's decision chain playing out in real
                time: detect -> model -> debate -> act -> survive
                (Each step follows logically from the last)
```

**Critical anti-pattern**: Do NOT start with "We built an AI agent that manages a Mars greenhouse." This is a feature description, not a story. It triggers analytical evaluation, not transportation. Start with the characters and their situation.

### Specific EDEN Tactic

Open with: "It's Sol 280. Commander Chen checks the greenhouse. Everything looks green. In 50 hours, everything will be dying." Then pause. Let the judges' minds fill in the gap. THEN show the system.

## 6. The Surprise Premium: Breaking the Pattern

### The Mechanism

**Novelty and the orienting response**: When the brain encounters something unexpected, the locus coeruleus fires a burst of norepinephrine, triggering the "orienting response" (Sokolov, 1963). This is INVOLUNTARY -- it cannot be suppressed. It produces:

1. **Attentional capture** -- all processing resources redirect to the novel stimulus
2. **Enhanced encoding** -- novel information is stored more effectively in memory (Ranganath & Rainer, 2003)
3. **Dopaminergic activation** -- the novelty itself is rewarding. fMRI studies show the substantia nigra/ventral tegmental area (SN/VTA) fires in response to novel stimuli, the same reward circuit activated by food and sex (Bunzeck & Duzel, 2006, Neuron)

**Prediction error**: The brain constantly predicts what will happen next. When prediction is violated, a "prediction error" signal fires (Schultz, 1997). This is the neurological basis of surprise. Larger prediction errors = stronger dopamine response = better memory encoding.

**Pattern-matching fatigue in judges**: After 15 teams of dashboards + slide decks, judges have a very strong internal prediction: "next team will show a dashboard and talk about features." When EDEN violates this pattern with a physical plant, LED effects, and a narrative about people dying on Mars, the prediction error is massive.

### What Breaks the Pattern in Hackathons

Based on the novelty research, the most powerful pattern breaks are:

1. **Sensory channel switch**: Everyone is visual (slides/screens). Adding physical (plant) and environmental (LED lighting change) breaks the sensory prediction.
2. **Emotional register shift**: Most teams are excited/enthusiastic. Starting with quiet tension ("It's Sol 280...") breaks the emotional prediction.
3. **Temporal frame shift**: Most teams present in present tense ("our app does X"). Opening in narrative past/future tense is unexpected.
4. **First-person vs. third-person**: Most teams say "we built." Starting with "Commander Chen" is a subject-shift that triggers orienting.

### Specific EDEN Tactic

The MAXIMUM prediction error sequence:
1. Team walks up. Plant is already on the table (judges think: "...what?")
2. Quiet, measured opening -- not the typical hackathon energy (emotional prediction error)
3. "It's Sol 280." (narrative frame, not feature frame -- structural prediction error)
4. Then, during the demo, the LIGHTS ON THE PLANT CHANGE. (environmental prediction error -- the physical world just responded to a software event)

Each prediction error compounds. By the time the plant's LEDs dim, judges have experienced 4 consecutive prediction violations. Their attention is fully captured, dopamine is flowing, and memory encoding is at maximum.

## 7. Social Proof in Panel Judging

### The Mechanism

Asch's conformity experiments (1951) demonstrated that individuals conform to group opinions even when those opinions are demonstrably wrong. In panel judging, this manifests as:

**Cascade effects** (Bikhchandani, Hirshleifer & Welch, 1992): Once one judge expresses a strong opinion, others tend to align. The first vocal judge creates an "information cascade" that amplifies or suppresses subsequent opinions.

**Anchoring in group deliberation** (Kahneman, Slovic & Tversky, 1982): The first number spoken becomes the anchor. If Judge A says "I'd give them an 8," Judge B's score will regress toward 8 regardless of their independent assessment.

**Champion effect**: In venture capital (Huang & Pearce, 2015), a single "champion" on the investment committee who advocates for a deal is the strongest predictor of funding. The champion provides social cover for others to agree.

### Creating Your Champion

Research on persuasion and influence (Cialdini, 2001) suggests targeting the judge most likely to become your champion:

1. **Identify the technical judge** vs. the business judge vs. the design judge. Each will champion different things.
2. **For EDEN: Target the judge most likely to be emotionally moved.** The champion doesn't need to be the most senior -- they need to be the most vocal and passionate.
3. **Eye contact creates alliance**: Research on gaze behavior (Kleinke, 1986) shows that sustained eye contact during key emotional moments creates a sense of shared experience and implicit alliance.

### How to Identify and Win Your Champion

During the pitch:
- **Scan for engagement signals**: Forward lean, nodding, note-taking, widened eyes
- **Lock onto that judge during the peak moment** (CME crisis): Direct eye contact during the emotional high point creates a shared emotional experience
- **Address that judge's likely concern domain**: If they seem technical, drop one precise technical detail. If they seem design-focused, reference the visual language.

### Specific EDEN Tactic

Assign roles: one presenter primarily addresses the panel, but the second team member (if two present) watches for the most engaged judge and directs the climactic moment toward them. "When the solar storm hits and the lights change, look at Judge X" -- whoever was leaning forward most during setup.

## 8. What Actual Hackathon Judges Say (Pattern Synthesis)

### Synthesized Patterns from Judge Perspectives

From extensive hackathon judging literature (DevPost judge guides, MLH resources, YCombinator demo day advice, and published judge retrospectives):

**What winners do that losers don't:**

1. **Show, don't tell**: Winners do live demos. Losers describe what their app "would" do. The single strongest predictor of winning is a working demo that judges can see functioning in real time.

2. **Problem-first, not solution-first**: Winners spend 30-40% of their time on the PROBLEM before showing any solution. By the time they show the solution, judges are already nodding. Losers open with "We built X" and judges never understand why it matters.

3. **One clear narrative thread**: Winners have one story. Losers try to show every feature. "We built a Mars greenhouse AI that autonomously handles solar storm emergencies" is a winning sentence. "We built an AI with 4 layers, 5 agent types, DynamoDB, React, and a Raspberry Pi" is a losing sentence.

4. **The "wow moment" is planned, not accidental**: Winners know exactly which 10 seconds will make judges go "whoa." They build the entire pitch around reaching that moment. Losers distribute impact evenly, which means no moment is memorable.

5. **Human stakes, not technical stakes**: "If this fails, astronauts go hungry for 6 months" beats "If this fails, the system throws an error" by an order of magnitude.

6. **Confidence, not arrogance**: Winners are measured and authoritative. They know their system. They don't oversell. They don't say "we're going to revolutionize." They say "here's what it does" and let judges draw conclusions.

7. **Clean ending**: Winners have a rehearsed final sentence. Losers trail off with "so... yeah, that's our project." The difference in perceived quality from a clean ending is disproportionate.

**Common judge complaints:**
- "I didn't understand what it does until minute 2 of 3"
- "They showed me every feature but I couldn't tell you what the product IS"
- "They said it works but didn't show it working"
- "Great tech, but I have no idea why anyone would use it"
- "They ran out of time and never showed the demo"

### Specific EDEN Tactic

Structure based on what judges actually reward:
- 0:00-0:30: Problem + human stakes (Mars, astronauts, food)
- 0:30-1:00: "What if the AI could handle it autonomously?" + live demo begins
- 1:00-2:15: The demo IS the pitch. Show the crisis unfolding in real-time. Plant LEDs changing. Agent council debating. The system responding.
- 2:15-2:45: Result -- the greenhouse survived. Show the outcome.
- 2:45-3:00: Clean final line. Rehearsed. Powerful. Done.

## 9. The 3-Second Rule: Winning Before You Start

### The Mechanism

Ambady & Rosenthal (1993) demonstrated "thin-slicing" -- people form accurate personality and competence judgments from as little as 2 seconds of observation. Their study of teacher evaluations showed that 2-second silent video clips predicted end-of-semester student ratings with r = 0.76. Two seconds. No audio.

**What thin-slices capture:**

1. **Posture**: Upright, open posture signals competence and confidence (Carney, Cuddy & Yap, 2010). Crossed arms, hunched shoulders, or fidgeting signals anxiety.

2. **Facial expression**: A genuine (Duchenne) smile -- one that reaches the eyes -- triggers unconscious positive evaluation. Forced smiles are detected subconsciously and create unease (Ekman, 2003).

3. **Movement confidence**: Deliberate, unhurried movement signals competence. Rushing signals anxiety. Walking to the front slowly and placing materials with intention creates authority.

4. **Voice (first word)**: Pitch, volume, and pace in the first syllable signal confidence or nervousness. Research by Scherer (1978) showed that listeners assess competence from vocal cues within 200ms.

5. **Environmental control**: Having materials set up, knowing where to stand, not fumbling with cables -- all signal preparation and competence before a word is spoken.

### The Willis & Todorov Study (2006, Psychological Science)

Even more extreme: participants formed trustworthiness and competence judgments from faces in 100 MILLISECONDS, and these snap judgments correlated r = 0.90 with judgments made with no time constraint. The first impression isn't just fast -- it's essentially the final impression unless actively overridden (which fatigued judges won't do).

### Specific EDEN First 3 Seconds

**Pre-pitch setup (before clock starts):**
- Plant is already on the table, LEDs glowing softly green. Judges notice it. Curiosity activates.
- Team walks up unhurried. Materials are ready. No fumbling.

**First 3 seconds of the pitch:**
- Presenter is already standing in position (not walking to the mic)
- FIRST WORDS should be low-pitch, measured, confident: "It's Sol 280."
- NOT "Hi, we're team EDEN and we built..."
- The in-medias-res opening signals mastery of the form

**The anti-pattern to avoid:**
"Hi everyone! So, uh, we're team EDEN, and we're really excited to show you what we built this weekend! So basically what we did is..."
This opening screams amateur. Every judge has heard it 15 times already. It triggers pattern-matching ("another enthusiastic team"), categorization ("mid-tier"), and partial tuning-out.

## 10. Multisensory Engagement: Visual + Auditory + Physical

### The Mechanism

**Multisensory integration research** (Stein & Meredith, 1993) demonstrates that the brain combines information from multiple senses SUPER-ADDITIVELY -- the combined effect is greater than the sum of individual channels.

**Key findings:**

- **Shams, Kamitani & Shimojo (2000)**: Cross-modal interactions can override single-channel perception. A single flash accompanied by two beeps is perceived as two flashes. The brain integrates, not just adds.

- **Murray et al. (2005)**: Multisensory stimuli are processed faster and more accurately than unisensory stimuli. Reaction time drops and accuracy increases when information comes through multiple channels simultaneously.

- **Nelson & Hitchon (1999)**: Advertisements with multisensory elements (sight + sound + touch) were rated as more persuasive, more memorable, and more credible than single-channel ads.

- **Morrin & Ratneshwar (2003)**: Adding touch to a brand experience increased brand recall by 30% and increased positive attitude formation.

**The "congruence premium"** (Spence, 2011): When multi-sensory signals are CONGRUENT (they tell the same story through different channels), credibility and memory encoding both increase. When they're incongruent (e.g., exciting music but calm visuals), confusion decreases impact.

### EDEN's Multisensory Advantage

Most hackathon teams use 1.5 channels:
- Visual (dashboard/slides)
- Auditory (presenter voice)
- That's it.

EDEN uses 3+ channels:
- **Visual**: Dashboard on screen
- **Auditory**: Narrator voice telling the story
- **Physical/Environmental**: Living plant with LED state changes
- **Environmental/Ambient**: The room's ambient perception changes when LED color shifts

This creates a multisensory integration event during the CME crisis that other teams literally cannot compete with. The LEDs dimming isn't a gimmick -- it's a neurologically-privileged encoding moment.

### The Congruence Principle Applied

Every channel must tell the same story at the same time:

| Moment | Visual (Screen) | Auditory (Voice) | Physical (Plant) |
|--------|----------------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Normal ops | Green dashboard, healthy metrics | Calm, measured narration | LEDs bright green, plant healthy |
| CME detected | Warning alerts appear | Voice: tension rises | LEDs begin amber shift |
| Crisis peak | Red alerts, agent council debating | Voice: "50 hours until impact" | LEDs red/dim, plant "stressed" |
| Resolution | Systems recovering, metrics improving | Voice: relief, confidence | LEDs returning to green |

When all channels shift simultaneously, the brain encodes this as a SINGLE powerful coherent experience, not three separate data streams. This is the super-additive effect.

### Specific EDEN Tactic

**Rehearse the synchronization.** The LED color change, the voice inflection change, and the dashboard state change must happen within 1-2 seconds of each other. Asynchrony breaks congruence and looks like a tech demo. Synchrony creates an EXPERIENCE.

## Strategic Synthesis: The Unified EDEN Pitch Strategy

### How All 10 Domains Compound

These aren't 10 separate tricks. They form a coherent psychological system:

```
BEFORE THE PITCH STARTS
  [9: First Impression] Plant is on table. Team is composed. Curiosity gap opens.
  [4: Embodied Cognition] Physical object breaks the screen-only pattern.
  [6: Surprise] First prediction error: "why is there a plant?"

SECONDS 0-30: THE HOOK
  [9: Thin-Slicing] Measured voice, "It's Sol 280." Competence signaled.
  [5: Transportation] Narrative frame, not feature frame. Defenses drop.
  [6: Surprise] Second prediction error: story opening, not product pitch.
  [2: Fatigue] Easy to process. One story. One thread. No cognitive load.

SECONDS 30-90: THE SETUP
  [5: Transportation] Characters, stakes, vivid imagery. Judges absorbed.
  [8: Judge Advice] Problem-first. 40% on why it matters before showing how.
  [3: Memorability] Emotional tagging begins. Stakes are human (hunger, survival).

SECONDS 90-135: THE PEAK
  [1: Peak-End] This IS the peak moment. Maximum emotional intensity.
  [10: Multisensory] All channels fire simultaneously: screen, voice, LEDs.
  [6: Surprise] Third prediction error: the physical world responds to software.
  [4: Embodied] The plant "suffers." Abstract AI becomes concrete, visceral.
  [2: Fatigue] Affect heuristic triggered. "This makes me FEEL something."
  [7: Social Proof] Lock eyes with most engaged judge during this moment.

SECONDS 135-165: THE RESOLUTION
  [5: Transportation] Story completes. "The greenhouse survived."
  [10: Multisensory] LEDs return to green. Congruent recovery across all channels.
  [3: Memorability] The "water cooler moment" is crystallized. Plant saved.

SECONDS 165-180: THE END
  [1: Peak-End] Rehearsed final line. Strong, clean, resonant.
  [8: Judge Advice] No trailing off. No "so yeah." Confident close.
  [7: Social Proof] The champion judge now has a story to tell the others.
```

### The Compound Effect

Each mechanism amplifies the others:
- **Surprise** (novelty) triggers **attention** (orienting response)
- **Attention** enables **transportation** (you can't be absorbed if you're not paying attention)
- **Transportation** disables **counter-arguing** (judges stop being critical)
- **Disabled counter-arguing** + **affect heuristic** (from fatigue) = **halo effect** across ALL scoring dimensions
- **Multisensory** encoding + **emotional arousal** + **narrative structure** = **maximum memorability**
- **Memorability** enables the **water cooler effect** which triggers **social proof** in deliberation
- **Peak-end rule** means the judges' memory of the experience is dominated by the crisis moment and the clean close

This isn't 10 separate advantages. It's a psychological flywheel where each mechanism increases the power of all the others.

### The One-Sentence Version

**Make judges FEEL the stakes of a Mars greenhouse crisis through synchronized physical-digital-narrative experience so vivid that they cannot help but remember it, talk about it, and score it high across all dimensions.**
